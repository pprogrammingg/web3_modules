Segment 1 — What people mean by “quantum cryptography”

Journalists and sales decks overload the phrase. In practice, distinguish at least:

  • Quantum key distribution (QKD) — physical-layer protocols (often photons) to agree symmetric keys with detection of many eavesdropping attempts. Still needs authenticated classical channels (pre-shared keys or post-quantum / classical signatures) to defeat person-in-the-middle.
  • Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) — classical algorithms conjectured resistant to quantum computers (signatures, KEMs). Software-only, runs on existing CPUs—this is what your PQC module targets.
  • Quantum random number generators (QRNG) — harvest entropy from quantum processes for seeding; useful operationally, not a replacement for protocols.

Blockchains and most FinTech APIs live in the PQC + classical world for integrity and key agreement at scale. QKD shows up in metro links, defense, regulated backhaul—different procurement and threat model.

Segment 2 — QKD in one architectural pass

BB84-style intuition: Alice encodes bits in randomly chosen bases; Bob measures in random bases; they publicly compare bases (not bits), discard mismatches, estimate error rate, then run error correction and privacy amplification to distill a key. If Eve couples strongly to the quantum channel, error rates spike—many designs abort or shrink key rate.

Security story: security is often argued from physical principles conditional on device and channel models. Implementations worry about detector blinding, side channels, trusted relays (quantum repeaters are hard), and finite-size effects (not asymptotic proofs).

Compared to ECDH, QKD tries to move some assurance from computational hardness to physics + correct lab hardware—trading math assumptions for engineering and distance constraints.

Segment 3 — Why this matters to protocol engineers (without magic)

  • Not a replacement for consensus or signatures. Validators still need digital signatures and hash links; QKD does not give you global ordering or Sybil resistance.
  • Hybrid stacks. A bank might explore QKD for a fixed fiber between two data centers and run Kyber/Dilithium-class algorithms on servers—orthogonal layers.
  • Harvest-now / decrypt-later still pushes you toward PQC for long-lived ciphertext; QKD secures new key material on a link, not retroactively hardening old TLS records.

Segment 4 — Limits and sane reading order

  • Distance & rate — attenuation kills key rate; trusted nodes or satellites change the trust model.
  • Authentication gap — QKD alone does not authenticate endpoints; you still need credentials or signatures.
  • Cost & ops — spare parts, calibration, monitoring—different from shipping a Rust binary with a PQC KEM.

Skim NIST PQC for software migration; for QKD, ETSI / ITU-T industry specs are the usual starting point when a customer mandates it.
